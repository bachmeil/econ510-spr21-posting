Answer: Consumption when young and old remain $400,000/2 = $200,000. GDP is unaffected when Janice is young because demand doesn't change. The fiscal policy of sending checks out to people is completely ineffective. This is precisely the Ricardian equivalence mechanism we discussed in class. Due to the severity of the recession, the government sends out an additional check for $20,000 when Janice is young. It will be paid for with $20,000 higher taxes when old. What is her consumption when young, assuming she still faces the credit constraint? What is her MPC out of the government check? Does fiscal policy have an effect on GDP when there's a recession and people face credit constraints?



Please enter your answer

Answer: As we know, her lifetime after tax income doesn't change because the check when young is offset by higher taxes when old. Her consumption smoothing level of consumption is still $100,000 when young. Her income when young is now $30,000. She consumes $30,000 because that is as close as she can get to her preferred consumption level.

She has $20,000 in additional income, and her consumption rises by $20,000. Her MPC out of the government check is 1.0. She's spends it all. Fiscal policy is extremely effective when it targets unemployed people with credit constraints. They will spend all or most of the check they get. That increases demand and GDP rises during the recession. Tax cuts probably won't be as effective as sending out checks, since somebody that's unemployed probably isn't paying taxes anyway, so the money will go to those that have no liquidity constraints. That is one reason why in 2020 the government sent out checks rather than cutting taxes.
