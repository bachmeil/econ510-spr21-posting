    Ricardian Equivalence

    You always have to account for the GBC when analyzing the effect of a change in fiscal policy.

    Spending = Tax revenue + Net Borrowing - Net Transfers

    When there's a tax cut, it has to be paid for by

    • Cutting spending
    • Increasing borrowing
    • Cutting transfers

    Cutting Spending

    Cut taxes to increase demand

    Spending (-100) = Tax revenue (-100) + Net Borrowing - Net Transfers

    Pay for it by cutting highway maintenance: Offsetting decrease in demand

    This makes no sense as a form of fiscal policy!

    Cutting transfers

    Cut taxes to increase demand

    Spending = Tax revenue (-100) + Net Borrowing - Net Transfers (-100)

    Pay for it by cutting Social Security payments by an equal amount: Offsetting decrease in demand

    This is also a policy that makes no sense!

    Borrow More

    Cut taxes to increase demand

    Spending = Tax revenue (-100) + Net Borrowing (+100) - Net Transfers

    Pay for it by selling bonds that will be repaid in 30 years.

    The only option for fiscal policy is if it involves government debt. No other fiscal policy can influence demand.

    Ricardian Equivalence

    The surprising result known as Ricardian equivalence says even the last option has no effect on demand.

    Borrowing implies future taxes to repay the debt.

    Tax cuts funded by borrowing are a change in the timing of taxes, not a change in your lifetime tax burden.

    Example

    A government agent hands you a $10 bill. It's yours to keep. In one minute, you have to repay the $10 or go to jail for the rest of your life.

    Would you spend more because of this?

    Why Ricardian equivalence fails

    In practice, Ricardian equivalence fails to some extent, and tax cuts do cause GDP to rise in the short run.

    • You might not expect to be alive when the tax bill comes due.
      • One minute vs one century
      • If you care about giving your kids an inheritance, you might care about policy in one century.
      • Most of us will never pay the inheritance tax so what happens in a century doesn't tax your wealth in the US.

    Most important: Liquidity constraints

    A liquidity constraint is a restriction on the amount you can buy.

    Example: College Students

    Income in 2021: 0

    Average income in 2022 and later: 80,000

     PIH plus consumption smoothing says you should borrow about $80,000 in 2021. Reality says nobody's going to lend you $80,000 because you want to increase your consumption.

    Your consumption is limited by your credit constraint. You can't borrow enough to get to the consumption smoothing level of consumption.

    Add government borrowing plus a check

    You can't borrow, but the government can borrow and send you the money, and that's just as good.

    Since you want to consume more, you'll spend the money, and demand and GDP will increase.

    A long-time argument against tuition subsidies is that students will spend the money on beer, pizza, and such.

    Important Qualification

    Ricardian equivalence is not only a statement that people will save a tax cut or transfer. They'll do so in order to pay their future taxes.

    There are lots of good reasons to save a tax cut/transfer even if you don't care about your future taxes.

    Profitability vs cash flow

    Profitable: Revenue coming in exceeds the cost of running the business over a long period of time.

    Cash flow: The amount of revenue flowing into a business versus the amount being paid out at a point in time.

    The difference is in the time units.
