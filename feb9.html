Chapter 3 develops what we call the goods market model. We want to develop a formal model of the amount all firms in the economy produce. That in turn determines the demand for workers and the amount that's consumed.
We assume that in the short run, firms will produce as much as is demanded. If people want to buy 10 more blue Ford Explorers, then Ford produces an additional 10 blue Ford Explorers.

Behold the Ford Explorer
Output is said to be demand determined.

You might think demand determined output is rather obvious. You might ask how anything else could ever possibly be the case.

Consider the case of a Taylor Swift concert.

Behold Taylor Swift
Taylor Swift is pretty popular.

If you have a Taylor Swift concert in the Manhattan High School gym, there are only a few seats available relative to the number of people that would like to attend. It would sell out immediately.

If the demand for Taylor Swift tickets increases, are they going to produce more? They can't, because there are no more tickets to make. In that case, they're going to raise the price, since they don't need to sell to everyone. They now have a few more crazy people that will pay an outrageous price for a ticket. This is an example of an output ceiling. It's not possible to increase production.
In other cases, firms might see a profit opportunity.

If a tornado knocks out power to thousands of people, some retailers might take advantage of the situation to double the price of generators. From an economic perspective, this is neither good nor bad, though you might have your own personal views on the morality of doing something like that.
This is an example where there's a lot of profit to be had from raising the price because there's not enough time to produce new generators, so the supply is fixed over the time frame people need generators.
In practice, neither of these scenarios seems to describe most of our interactions. We mostly don't see fixed supply or output ceilings.

Firms tend to change prices slowly when demand changes. If firms raise their price when demand rises, there's no reason to expect changes in demand to lead to changes in production. If you look at the data, you don't see prices rising when demand rises.

One of the reasons cited by managers is that raising prices makes customers upset. They don't want to try to profit from a small surge in demand, because everyone that pays higher prices today will curse them and buy from a competitor in the future.
This is the observation of sticky prices.

That observation is the foundation for everything we'll do in chapters 3-5. We make the assumption that firms don't change prices at all when there's a change in demand. We'll relax that assumption later in the semester.

The equilibrium condition is that GDP, the amount firms produce, is exactly equal to the amount demanded. We can draw the equilibrium as what you might have heard called the 45-degree line.
We've now defined all of the possible equilibrium amounts of production. That only tells us which levels of production can be an equilibrium. We now need to describe the behavior underlying demand so we can determine which level of production will be chosen.
Demand equation Z = C + I + G
It turns out that demand depends on GDP. A higher level of GDP means disposable income is higher, which in turn means consumption is higher. We can define the aggregate consumption function in terms of GDP. The aggregate consumption function differs from the one you saw in the module in two ways:
  • It describes all consumption in the economy rather than consumption for one person.
  • The aggregate MPC can be different from the individual MPC (and indeed, is different, because everyone has their own MPC). The aggregate MPC is how much aggregate consumption rises if GDP rises by $1. It has to be less than one.
I'm going to deviate just a bit from the book to make this distinction clear. I'm going to use the aggregate consumption function

C = a + b Y

where C is aggregate consumption, a is aggregate autonomous consumption, b is the aggregate MPC, and Y is GDP. The author does not have a or b in his aggregate consumption function.
The equation for total demand depends on its three components:

Z = C + I + G

Plugging in from the previous slide for consumption, we have:

Z = a + bY + I + G

so now demand depends on the value of GDP.
Suppose we have (in billions)

C = 100 + 0.75Y
I = 300
G = 100

Then demand is given by (using some middle school calculations)

Z = 100 + 0.75Y + 300 + 100
Z = (100 + 300 + 100) + 0.75Y
Z = 500 + 0.75Y
