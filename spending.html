Overview The goal of this problem set is to demonstrate that Ricardian equivalence, according to which tax cuts and transfers have no effect on GDP, does not imply that government spending has no effect on GDP. There are two important concepts to understand: production shifting and government expenditure shifting.

Answer: In this case, there is government expenditure when young that causes taxes to rise when young. There is nothing offsetting the higher taxes, so lifetime after tax income falls.

Consumption when young and old falls to $99,500. The higher taxes from building the bridge cause consumption to fall, hurting demand. Total consumption is now 1000($99,500) = $99,500,000 or $500,000 less when young and when old.



Continuing on from the previous question, calculate total demand in Stubville when young and old. Does this fiscal policy affect GDP?



Please enter your answer

Answer: Unlike the earlier question, there are now two components of demand: consumption and government spending. We know C is $99,500,000 when young and old. G is $1,000,000 when young and $0 when old. Total demand is $99,500,000 + $1,000,000 = $100,500,000 when young and $99,500,000 + $0 = $99,500,000 when old.

Fiscal policy (the government spending on the bridge) does have an effect on demand and therefore GDP. It increases demand when young and decreases demand by an equivalent amount when old. This is known as production shifting. Some of the production when old has been shifted into the young period due to government spending. The intution behind this is that 100% of the government spending takes place when young, but the residents pay for it by lowering their consumption by the same amount every time period. That means fiscal policy can affect demand.

You might wonder how this matters. GDP will be higher when young but lower when old. It would seem that fiscal policy is useless. Recall, however, that the goal of fiscal policy is to increase demand when the economy is in a recession. Government spending can achieve that quite simply if it takes place during a recession. Even though you're not increasing GDP over long periods of time, you're increasing GDP during the time periods it's valuable to raise GDP.



What happens if Stubville had been planning to build the bridge when old but then moved it to young when the recession hit?



Please enter your answer

Answer: This is sort of a fake question to motivate what I'm about to tell you. If Stubville was planning to build the bridge when old, the residents of Stubville had already reduced their consumption to cover the cost. If they move the building of the bridge to young, demand and GDP rise when young (as before) but there's no change in consumption in either time period as a result of building the bridge earlier. In this sense, you can increase GDP during a recession using government purchases without any negative side effects. Changing the timing of government expenditures to stimulate the economy during a recession is known as government expenditure shifting. A similar thing happens in reverse if you postpone government expenditures when the economy is strong.
