1. You have $10,000 in savings. You want to hold the savings for one year. You are considering holding a US bond or holding a German bond. The US interest rate is 5%. If you buy the US bond, how many dollars will you have after one year?

Answer 1.05($10,000) = $10,5000

2. You decide to buy the German bond. Suppose the exchange rate is such that you have to give up $1.25 to get one euro.

  • a. How many euros worth of bonds can you buy today with your $10,000?
  • b. How many euros will you have on repayment in one year if the German interest rate is 5%?
  • c. How many dollars will you have in one year if you convert the proceeds from (b) into dollars at an exchange rate of $1.25 per euro.

    • Answer a. 8000 euros b. 1.05(8000) = 8400 euros c. $1.25/euro(8400 euros) = $10,500

    IS/LM and the Exchange Rate

    • Cut the money supply (LM shifts up)
    • Send checks to everyone (IS shifts right)
    • Lower consumer confidence (IS shifts left)

    ➀ Interest rate determines the demand for the dollar. High demand means E rises.

    ➁ Prices of goods don't change in the short run, so the real exchange rate rises.

    ➂ High real exchange rate hurts exporters.

    US Imports

    What determines how much the US imports from other countries?

    • Real exchange rate
    • US GDP (total US consumption)

    As the US economy expands, we purchase more goods made in other countries.

    Expanding imports is generally a sign that the US economy is strong. The opposite for weak import numbers.

    Business confidence matters: If we're producing lots of investment goods, that output can't be consumed.

    You can fill the "consumption gap" this leaves by importing.

    Exports: If we're producing export goods (agriculture) then we're not consuming those goods. Imports will again fill that gap.

    https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/IMPGSC1

    Of course, the real exchange rate matters a lot too: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RBUSBIS

    Changes in the real exchange rate are typically different: They cause a shift from domestic production to imports and vice versa.

    Spillovers

    International Spillover: Effect of a shock in one economy has an effect on other economies.

    An expansion of the US economy causes Canadian GDP to rise.

    Goods market model for Canada: Demand shifts up, causing GDP to rise.

    US Exports

    What determines how much the US imports from other countries?

    • Real exchange rate
    • Foreign GDP (total foreign consumption)

    Exports are more sensitive to the real exchange rate since they go to many other countries.

    Goods market model for the US if Canada's economy grows.

    Goods market model for the US if the real exchange rate rises.

    Monetary policy is extremely important for US exporters

    International Business Cycles

    These spillovers lead to an international business cycle. Countries* tend to have similar recessions and expansions due to international trade.

    * Other than Australia.

    Incentive to Wait

    Spillovers introduce a problem for monetary policy in small open countries.

    Suppose the economy of Honduras goes into a recession. They can:

    • Increase the money supply: Higher GDP, more imports
    • Wait for the US to increase the money supply: Higher GDP, more exports

    Consider fiscal policy in Europe.

    Policy coordination: Countries promise to use policy to help the world/regional economy.

    These agreements lack an enforcement mechanism.

    Common Currency Area

    A common currency area is a region (typically a group of countries) that use the same currency.

    Countries like Ecuador and El Salvador use the US dollar as their currency. CCA with the US because you can travel there without changing your money.

    What are the advantages?

    • You can travel to Ecuador without the cost of currency conversion.
    • There is no exchange rate risk for US companies doing business in Ecuador.

    What's the disadvantage? Ecuador cannot use monetary policy if it goes into a recession.

    Whether to share a currency depends heavily on how monetary policy is set and the amount of risk if you don't.

    The optimum currency area is the currency area that makes the most sense for everyone.

    Debt Crises

    Government borrowing:

  • Low existing debt levels (no risk)
  • Moderate debt levels (slight risk)
  • Extremely high debt levels (certain default): Crisis!

    • Moderate debt + large recession: Get rid of those bonds.

    Case of Greece: Get rid of bonds and the euro.

    Greek debt selloff would spill over to all other euro-using countries.
